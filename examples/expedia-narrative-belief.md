# Narrative Understanding Belief — Expedia Group

*Source: Q1 2025 · Q2 2025 · Q3 2025 · Q4 2025 · Q1 2026 earnings call summaries*
*Five quarters of earnings communications read as a single evolving pattern.*

---

### The Three Priorities as a Permanent Frame

Since Q2 2024 — Ariane Gorin's first full quarter as CEO — Expedia's earnings communications have operated inside a fixed three-part strategic framework: deliver more value for travelers, invest where we see the greatest opportunity for growth, and drive operating efficiencies and expand margins. By Q1 2025 these three priorities were already well-established language. Across the five quarters now covered by this belief, they have been repeated in the same sequence, using the same words, in every single call.

The persistence of the language is more notable than the content of the priorities themselves. In Q1 2025, Gorin stated all three in the context of a quarter where consumer bookings grew only 1% and Hotels.com went negative. In Q2 2025 — a stronger quarter — the same three priorities were stated again, unchanged. In Q3 2025, with bookings accelerating to 12%, the framing was the same. In Q4 2025, with guidance of 6-8% growth for 2026, the same three. In Q1 2026, with the highest Q1 EBITDA margin in 15 years, the language was identical: "delivering more value to travelers, investing where we see the greatest opportunities, driving operating efficiencies and margin expansion."

The framework has now survived three distinct demand environments — the macro softness and consumer weakness of Q1 2025, the recovery of Q2-Q3 2025, and the strong execution narrative of Q4 2025 and Q1 2026. A strategic framework that is equally applicable to all three conditions is not a framework in the analytical sense — it is a communication container. It provides consistent vocabulary without providing a definition of what success within any of the three pillars looks like, what would indicate failure, or what would cause the framework to be replaced.

The three priorities have also never been given metrics. "Deliver more value for travelers" is not defined with a traveler satisfaction score, a retention rate, or a repeat booking percentage that would allow confirmation in a subsequent quarter. "Drive operating efficiencies" is closest to measurable — EBITDA margin expansion has been named with basis points in each quarter — but the target has shifted (50 bps in Q4 2024, 75-100 bps in Q1 2025, then over 2 points actual in Q3 2025, then 100-125 bps guidance for 2026) without any reference to prior targets. The framework absorbs all outcomes.

**Confidence: 0.88 | Direction: Stable**

### How Underperforming Businesses Are Language-Managed

A consistent and measurable pattern across five quarters is the vocabulary Expedia uses to communicate about brands and segments that are underperforming. The pattern is not silence — management names underperforming units directly. The pattern is the systematic deployment of minimizing qualifiers that describe the direction of a result without naming its magnitude, converting a weak outcome into a narrative of relative progress.

Vrbo is the most persistent example. In Q1 2025, Vrbo "grew modestly for the third consecutive quarter" — the word "modestly" appears without a number, without a comparison to the market, without a prior definition of what modest means. In Q2 2025, Vrbo "experienced bookings decline" — the language shifted to acknowledge a negative without quantifying it. The explanation was structural: "lower daily rates, shorter length of stay and higher cancellations in the softer environment." In Q3 2025, Vrbo was positioned as a "vacation rental pure play" with "20% of bookings on partner-funded promotional rates" — a metric that reframes the scale of promotional dependence as a product capability. By Q4 2025, the three consumer brands were described as each having a distinct identity: Expedia as "one stop shop," Hotels.com as "hotel pure play," Vrbo as "trusted pure play vacation rental marketplace." The pure play framing is notable — it positions Vrbo's narrower focus as a feature, not a constraint.

Hotels.com followed the same vocabulary pattern. In Q1 2025 it "slipped back into negative territory" — the slippage language implies an accident rather than a persistent condition. In Q2 2025, following its April relaunch, Hotels.com "showed recovery signs" — directional language without a number. Gorin acknowledged it had been "our most disrupted of all of our brands from the platform migrations" — a structural explanation that locates the cause in a prior event. By Q4 2025, Hotels.com was described as achieving "fastest pace in over 2 years" — a superlative framed against a time window that does not reveal the absolute level.

The consistent feature across both brands across all five quarters: the language names the direction but not the magnitude. A result that would invite scrutiny if stated as a number is instead stated as a direction with a qualifier. "Grew modestly," "recovery signs," "fastest pace in over 2 years," "pure play" — each of these is a true description that simultaneously contains the result and softens its accountability surface.

**Confidence: 0.85 | Direction: Stable**

### AI Attribution: Expanding Frame, Contracting Specificity

Across five quarters, the role AI plays in Expedia's communications has followed a pattern visible in other technology-era earnings calls: the frame expands while the specific attribution contracts. In Q1 2025, the frame was already broad — "AI is changing everything and we see it as an accelerator across our business" — but it was accompanied by named integrations: OpenAI operator, Microsoft Copilot Actions, Instagram Trip Matching. The specificity of the partnerships gave the frame a surface accountability.

By Q2 2025, the AI language had become operational — "AI filters," "personalized insurance resulting in our highest insurance attach rates ever," AI-reduced engineering cycle times "by more than 20% in some teams." The claims were more measurable than the prior quarter's partnership names — attach rates and cycle times are internal metrics — but the causal link between AI and the metric was stated directionally, not demonstrated. A 20% cycle time reduction attributed to AI is a measurable claim; what is absent is the prior baseline, the methodology, and whether the improvement is sustained.

By Q3 2025, the language had shifted to deployment scale: "virtual agents resolve over 50% of traveler queries." This is the first AI claim across the five quarters that carries a specific scale metric. It is also a claim about cost reduction and capacity — resolving queries via AI is primarily a margin lever, not a demand driver. The shift from AI as demand accelerant to AI as cost efficiency vehicle happened without explicit acknowledgment.

By Q4 2025, the language bifurcated. On the external product side: "personalization," "better recommendations," "better ranking models" — directional claims with no metrics. On the internal efficiency side: "AI to give our team superpowers," "shortening cycle time." The external AI frame remained aspiration; the internal AI frame was being held to some degree of accountability.

By Q1 2026, the frame reached its most specific form and its most expansive form simultaneously. Specific: "over 30% of 250 million annual service interactions powered by AI," agent onboarding time reduced "by about 60%," ChatGPT and Claude integrations generating "hundreds of millions of dollars in realized marketing value." Expansive: "AI reinforces our core advantages and amplifies our execution." The specific claims are on the cost and efficiency side. The expansive claim — AI reinforces core advantages — is on the competitive positioning side and carries no metric.

The pattern across five quarters: AI efficiency claims (cost, speed, resolution rates) have become increasingly specific and measurable. AI demand and competitive positioning claims ("accelerator across the business," "reinforces our core advantages") have remained expansive and unfalsifiable. The internal AI story is earning specificity; the external AI story is not.

**Confidence: 0.79 | Direction: Improving**

### Macro Attribution: Calibrated Acknowledgment Under Pressure

Expedia entered Q1 2025 having spent multiple prior quarters attributing results primarily to internal execution. The pattern shifted in Q1 2025 when consumer bookings grew only 1% — at that point, macro language entered the call explicitly: "weaker than expected travel demand in the US and into the US," "inbound bookings from Canada fell nearly 30%," "step down in both intra US and into the US dynamics." The Canada figure — 30% — was notable for its precision relative to the vague language typically used around consumer weakness.

Across Q2 and Q3 2025, as results recovered, the macro attribution receded. Q2 2025 described the U.S. travel market as "muted" with "consumers at the higher end remaining resilient and those at the lower end taking a more cautious approach" — softer macro framing than Q1, consistent with improving but not strong results. Q3 2025 raised guidance and described "an improved demand environment" — the macro frame had shifted from headwind to tailwind without explicit acknowledgment that the prior caution was now resolved.

By Q4 2025, macro language returned in a hedging form for forward guidance: "remaining appropriately cautious given ongoing macro uncertainty." The language was used prospectively rather than retrospectively — to qualify forward commitments rather than to explain past results. Q4 2025 actual results were strong; the macro caution applied to 2026.

Q1 2026 brought the most specific macro acknowledgment across five quarters. The Middle East conflict and Mexico travel advisories were named, quantified ("approximately 2 points" of impact for the full quarter, "roughly 5 points in March alone"), and given a resolution date ("cancellations stabilized in early April"). This is the most accountable macro attribution in five quarters — a specific event, a specific impact estimate, and a specific recovery signal. It is also notable that this level of specificity appeared in a quarter where results were strong (13% bookings growth, 15% revenue growth). The specificity of the macro acknowledgment in Q1 2026 is not defensive — it is offered from a position of strength, which makes it more credible as a genuine disclosure than the vague macro language of weaker quarters.

The pattern across five quarters: macro attribution appears and becomes specific when results are weak (Q1 2025) or when specific events make precision unavoidable (Q1 2026). It recedes to general language when results recover. The calibration of macro disclosure to result quality is the belief — not that macro conditions are misrepresented, but that the specificity of attribution tracks the communication need rather than the underlying volatility.

**Confidence: 0.74 | Direction: Stable**

### B2B as the Counter-Narrative to Consumer Weakness

Across five quarters, Expedia has consistently deployed B2B performance as the structural counterweight to consumer weakness — a communication pattern that presents the business as more resilient than any single segment result would suggest. The pairing is now reliable enough that the appearance of weak consumer results can be anticipated to be immediately followed by B2B strength as the mitigating frame.

In Q1 2025, consumer bookings grew 1%. The call pivoted immediately to B2B: "B2B bookings surged 14% with strong international performance, particularly in APAC which grew 30%." The 30% APAC figure gave the counter-narrative the same numerical specificity that was applied to the Canada decline. Two 30% numbers in the same call — one a decline, one a growth — and the growth number was placed after the decline in a way that absorbed it.

In Q2 2025, B2B delivered its "16th consecutive quarter of double-digit growth" — a streak framing that converts quarterly performance into a durable pattern claim. The streak language removes B2B from quarter-to-quarter evaluation and repositions it as a structural growth engine with demonstrated consistency. By Q3 2025, it was the "17th consecutive quarter." By Q4 2025, B2B grew 24%. By Q1 2026, B2B bookings reached $10.7 billion, up 22%, with the Uber partnership announced as a new structural win.

The communication effect of the streak framing: it makes B2B performance difficult to evaluate critically in any individual quarter because the relevant metric is the streak, not the quarter. A quarter where B2B grows 17% (slower than recent quarters) is still "another quarter of double-digit growth" — the streak absorbs the deceleration. The consecutive-quarter framing is most valuable to management in periods of deceleration, where the streak provides a floor that individual quarter performance cannot.

The B2B counter-narrative also serves a specific function when analysts press on U.S. consumer competitive positioning — a recurring analyst concern across all five quarters. When consumer performance lags competitors, B2B growth is offered as evidence of platform strength that is not visible in the consumer segment alone. The implication is that Expedia's competitive position should be evaluated across both segments. This is a legitimate argument; it is also a consistent deflection from the consumer competitiveness question.

**Confidence: 0.82 | Direction: Stable**
