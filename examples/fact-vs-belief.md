# Fact vs Belief

The same observation. Two treatments. One is correct.

---

## The Observation

From a quarterly review:

> "Customer acquisition cost for the period was $142, up from $118
> in the prior period — an increase of approximately 20%. The increase
> reflects higher media spend and increased competition for digital
> inventory in Q3."

---

## Filed As A Fact (Correct)

**Where it goes:** Archive + factual understanding tracker

Metric: Customer acquisition cost
Period: Q3 Cycle 7 | Value: $142 | Prior: $118 | Change: +20%
Attribution: Seasonal media market, Q3 digital competition

Signal note: One period of cost increase. Management attribution is
plausible and testable. If Q4 shows acquisition cost returning toward
$118–120, the attribution holds and no belief is needed. If cost
continues to rise in Q4 and Q1 without seasonal explanation, the
signal becomes a pattern — and the question then is what that pattern
implies about the business's growth channel economics.

**Watching next period.**

---

## Held As A Belief (Wrong)

Belief: "Customer acquisition cost is rising, suggesting the primary
growth channel is approaching diminishing returns."
Confidence: 0.20 | Direction: Deteriorating

**Why this is wrong:**

One data point is not a pattern. "Is rising" will be falsified the
moment acquisition cost declines in Q4 — which means this is a
tracked metric wearing a belief label, not a belief.

The interpretation ("approaching diminishing returns") is a structural
claim that requires observing the relationship between cost and volume
across multiple periods and market conditions. That evidence does not
exist yet. The belief is ahead of the evidence.

**The harm:** when Q4 shows acquisition cost at $128 (up, but less
than Q3), the system treats this as contradicting the belief.
Confidence drops. The world model now has a noise artifact — a belief
formed and partially contradicted — that will interfere with the real
signal if genuine acquisition cost deterioration develops over the
next year.

---

## The Rule

A fact describes what happened. It is always accepted and filed.

A belief describes what a recurring pattern means — durably and
falsifiably. It is earned over multiple observations.

If a single new data point would immediately contradict the belief,
it is not a belief. It is a fact being tracked.
