# Fact vs Belief — The Same Observation, Two Treatments

This file shows one raw observation handled two ways — once correctly
(filed as a fact) and once incorrectly (held as a belief). The
difference matters because a wrongly held belief will corrupt future
reasoning by treating a data point as an interpretation.

---

## The Raw Observation

From a quarterly review document:

> "Customer acquisition cost for the period was $142, up from $118
> in the prior period, an increase of approximately 20%. The increase
> reflects higher media spend and increased competition for digital
> inventory in Q3."

---

## Treatment A — Correct (Filed As A Fact)

**Filed to:** L3 Archive + Factual Understanding tracker

**Archive entry:**
Date: Q3 Cycle 7
Metric: Customer acquisition cost
Value: $142
Prior period: $118
Change: +$24 (+20%)
Attribution in document: Higher media spend, increased digital
competition in Q3

**Factual understanding signal note:**
Acquisition cost rose 20% in one period. Management attributes to
seasonal media market and competition. Single observation. No prior
pattern of sustained acquisition cost increase in this business.
Tracking as a signal. No belief formed.

**Why this is correct:**
— One period of movement does not establish a pattern
— The attribution (Q3 media market) is plausible and testable
— The number is a fact: it happened, it is dated, it is filed
— A belief would require the interpretation of what this implies
  about the business's growth economics — that is not yet visible
  from one data point

**What happens next:**
If acquisition cost rises again in Q4 and Q1 without seasonal
explanation, the signal becomes a pattern. Then the question becomes:
what does this pattern imply about the growth engine? That answer —
if falsifiable and distinctive — becomes a belief in business memory.

---

## Treatment B — Incorrect (Held As A Belief)

**Filed to:** Business Memory, BM-07 (incorrect)

**Belief statement:**
"Customer acquisition cost is rising, suggesting the business's
primary growth channel is becoming more expensive and less efficient."
Confidence: 0.35 | Direction: Deteriorating | Evidence: 1

**Why this is wrong:**

1. **One data point is not a pattern.** A belief requires recurrence.
   A single quarter's acquisition cost movement is a fact, not a
   durable interpretation.

2. **The management explanation is plausible and not yet contradicted.**
   Q3 media market competition is a real phenomenon. The belief is
   asserting structural channel deterioration when the evidence
   supports only a seasonal cost variation.

3. **The statement fails the falsifiability test properly applied.**
   The belief says "is rising" — but if the same metric returns to
   $120 in Q4, the belief was never valid. A belief must be about
   something durable enough that a single reversal does not instantly
   disprove it. This statement is too tied to one measurement.

4. **The implication is not yet earned.** "Primary growth channel
   becoming more expensive and less efficient" is a significant
   structural claim. It requires the pattern — the trend — not the
   single point. The interpretation is ahead of the evidence.

**The harm of holding this incorrectly:**
The next document that shows acquisition cost declining slightly will
appear to contradict a belief. The system will update downward. But
the belief was never valid — the update is noise applied to a
non-belief. The world model now has a record of a belief being
"contradicted" that never existed, which corrupts the confidence
history.

---

## The Rule This Illustrates

**Facts describe what happened.** They are always accepted. They are
dated and filed. They do not expire unless replaced by a correction.

**Beliefs describe what it means.** They require a pattern of
recurrence, a falsifiable interpretation, and specificity to this
business. They are earned over multiple observations.

**The test:** could I write this in a single sentence that a future
document could contradict in a meaningful way? If the answer changes
with every new data point, it is a fact being tracked, not a belief
being held.

A fact: "Acquisition cost was $142 in Q3, up 20%."
A belief: "Acquisition cost has risen for five consecutive periods
despite stable media market conditions, suggesting the core growth
channel is losing efficiency as competition for the same pool of
customers intensifies."

The second statement requires five periods, a comparison to market
conditions, and a mechanism. It is earned. The first is filed.
